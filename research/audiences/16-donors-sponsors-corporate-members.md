# Financial supporters: individual donors, corporate sponsors, and DSF corporate members

## Audience definition and boundaries

This is a **funding context occupied by different people and organizations**, not one persona. It includes individuals giving their own money; employees initiating employer matching; companies funding a dependency, ecosystem, or maintainer; and organizations entering a formal corporate-member relationship with the Django Software Foundation (DSF). Their shared behavior is transferring financial or in-kind resources, but their jobs, rights, evidence needs, and approval processes differ.

The relationship terms matter:

| Relationship | What is exchanged | Governance or control |
|---|---|---|
| Individual donation to the DSF | A personal gift, one-time or recurring | Does **not** purchase DSF individual membership. DSF individual membership recognizes substantial contribution and is not for sale. |
| DSF individual membership | Recognition of an individual's material contribution | Eligibility to vote for the DSF Board and Steering Council; financially donating is neither necessary nor sufficient. |
| DSF corporate membership | Board-approved, renewable one-year membership in return for an annual financial contribution; current tiers add recognition and promotional benefits | Current DSF bylaws give paid-up corporate members member voting rights. This is more than a logo-only sponsorship, although the fundraising pages also use “sponsor,” “member,” and “donor” language. It does not buy technical control. |
| Direct project/maintainer sponsorship | Money to a person or project, often through a platform and sometimes with tier benefits | No governance right should be assumed. Terms, recipient, tax status, and accountability vary. |
| Event or campaign sponsorship | Funding tied to an event, activity, or promotion | Usually purchases defined event/campaign benefits, not foundation or framework governance. It is adjacent, not the core relationship studied here. |
| In-kind support | Hosting, services, staff time, tooling, or other non-cash value | Rights depend on the agreement; value and conflicts require separate accounting. |

These distinctions are direct DSF facts plus cross-foundation comparison. The [DSF bylaws](https://www.djangoproject.com/foundation/records/bylaws/) define corporate membership and voting; the [individual-members page](https://www.djangoproject.com/foundation/individual-members/) defines earned individual membership; Apache explicitly separates sponsorship donations from ordinary donations, while Eclipse sells membership levels with differing governance rights ([ASF Sponsorship](https://www.apache.org/foundation/sponsorship.html), accessed 18 July 2026; [Eclipse Membership](https://www.eclipse.org/membership/), accessed 18 July 2026). **High confidence.** The practical exercise of DSF corporate-member voting rights and corporate members' awareness of them are not evidenced.

The boundary excludes choosing Django as a framework, buying Django consulting/support, advertising generally, applying for a grant, and contributing labor except where they trigger or substitute for financial support. A company may be both adopter and funder, but “fund Django” is not the same job as “standardize on Django.”

## Important subsegments

- **Individual user-beneficiary.** A developer, founder, consultant, educator, or former learner gives because Django created personal or professional value and money is easier to contribute than sustained volunteer time.
- **Individual community/cause donor.** The connection may be to free software, a welcoming global developer community, security and maintenance as public infrastructure, or access and inclusion rather than to a current production dependency.
- **Individual recurring, one-time, or campaign donor.** Recurring giving supports continuity; a one-time gift can express thanks or respond to a milestone, appeal, security event, or match. These are modes, not fixed identities.
- **Employee donor using workplace giving or matching.** The person chooses the cause, but an employer platform, eligibility rule, match window, and verification process mediate the gift.
- **Dependency-stewardship corporate supporter.** Engineers can identify that products or operations depend on Django; an OSPO, engineering leader, or security leader turns that dependence into a continuity, security, or reciprocity case.
- **Ecosystem/brand corporate supporter.** Developer relations, recruiting, partnerships, or marketing values credible association with a developer community. Visibility is a legitimate job, but it is different from dependency risk reduction.
- **Mission, responsibility, or executive-led supporter.** A founder or senior leader treats contribution as company culture, reciprocity, or social responsibility. Interview research with 17 companies found founder vision, reputation, business advantage, and reciprocity as distinct motivations ([“Rules of Engagement,”](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.08266) Guizani et al., 14 March 2023). **Direct qualitative evidence; Medium confidence.**
- **Large-enterprise corporate member.** Initiation, technical validation, budget ownership, vendor onboarding, tax/accounting, legal review, brand review, and final authorization may be distributed. An OSPO may coordinate them, but many companies have no OSPO.
- **Small-company corporate member.** A founder/CTO may combine champion, evaluator, economic buyer, and approver; an accountant or operations person still handles payment and tax records.
- **Direct maintainer/project sponsor.** This can target a person or narrow dependency more precisely than DSF funding. It should not be conflated with funding the DSF's Fellowship, grants, governance, or community work.

These segments overlap. Company size, motive, funding target, relationship type, and decision authority are better segmentation variables than a single “corporate sponsor” label.

## Primary job to be done

> When software or a community I value depends on work the market does not reliably pay for, I want to direct an affordable and trustworthy share of my resources toward its continuity, so I can help preserve the value I receive without needing to perform all of that work myself.

This is a **researcher inference; High confidence in the functional core, Medium in the wording**. OSS-specific studies directly establish gratitude for used software, organizational reciprocity/business motives, underpaid maintenance, and dependency-oriented funding. No reviewed study asked DSF donors to formulate a JTBD.

## Additional jobs to be done

There are six important jobs in total, including the primary job.

1. **Sustain valued shared infrastructure (primary).** When Django or its ecosystem creates value I want to preserve, I want to convert reliance or appreciation into resources for maintenance, security, releases, community operations, and continuity, so I can help sustain the shared infrastructure without having to perform all of that work myself.
2. **Reciprocate and affirm belonging as an individual.** When Django has helped me and time, expertise, or access limits other contributions, I want a proportionate way to express thanks and participate in its future, so I can reciprocate without taking on an unsuitable commitment.
3. **Make dependency stewardship fundable inside a company.** When organizational reliance creates a continuity or stewardship concern, I want to translate that reliance into a bounded proposal a budget owner, finance, legal, and leadership can assess, so I can obtain and renew appropriate support rather than assume dependence will fund itself.
4. **Earn credible association with an ecosystem.** When an organization wants a legitimate relationship with the Django ecosystem, I want support to produce clearly bounded recognition or relationship benefits, so I can advance developer trust, employer reputation, product complementarity, or visibility without pretending support buys technical influence.
5. **Choose the right funding relationship and target.** When several financial and in-kind support mechanisms are available, I want to match the target, rights, restrictions, and accountability to my desired outcome, so I can choose a relationship that funds the intended work without creating false expectations.
6. **Verify accountability while preserving project autonomy.** When I consider or renew support, I want to understand use of funds, governance, conflicts, results, and legal status, so I can verify stewardship without turning support into a demand for features, roadmap control, or privileged technical treatment.

## Functional, emotional, and social dimensions

| Job | Core functional job | Emotional job | Social job | Supporting jobs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sustain infrastructure | Move resources to work that reduces continuity and maintenance gaps | Reduce unease about benefiting from unpaid or fragile work (**inference**) | Be a responsible participant rather than a passive beneficiary (**inference**) | Identify dependence; select recipient; choose amount/cadence; renew |
| Reciprocate/belong | Convert gratitude into a practical contribution | Feel useful, grateful, and connected (**direct general/OSS evidence**) | Signal membership in a community one values (**hypothesis**) | Trust recipient; protect privacy; use matching; receive confirmation |
| Fund stewardship internally | Establish need, fit, cost, approval, and evidence | Reduce fear that an unusual spend will look naïve or indefensible (**hypothesis**) | Be seen internally as a prudent technical steward (**inference**) | Dependency evidence; proposal; budget; procurement; accounting; renewal |
| Earn association | Obtain truthful, proportionate recognition or access attached to support | Feel proud of the company's public commitment (**hypothesis**) | Demonstrate reciprocity and an open-source identity to developers and peers (**direct qualitative evidence**) | Brand review; benefit selection; attribution; engagement measurement |
| Choose relationship/target | Match instrument and recipient to intended outcome | Reduce regret about funding the wrong layer or creating influence concerns (**hypothesis**) | Avoid being seen as buying control or performative generosity (**hypothesis**) | Compare rights; tax status; restrictions; platform fees; contracts |
| Verify accountability/autonomy | Assess governance, finances, progress, independence, and conflicts | Feel confident rather than exploited or complicit (**inference**) | Maintain legitimacy with donors, employees, and the community (**inference**) | Reports; filings; minutes; policies; outcome review; conflict disclosure |

The functional jobs are **Medium-to-High confidence**. Gratitude is directly supported by GitHub Sponsors research; corporate reputation, reciprocity, and business advantage are directly supported by company interviews. Other emotional/social formulations are **Low-to-Medium-confidence hypotheses** requiring DSF donor interviews.

## Triggering situations

**Event-triggered**

- An individual solves a costly problem with Django, ships a product, earns income, learns a skill, or receives community help.
- A visible fundraising campaign, anniversary, matching period, release, security incident, maintainer departure, or sustainability discussion makes normally invisible work salient.
- An employee discovers employer matching or workplace giving eligibility.
- A dependency inventory, architecture/security review, incident, or executive question exposes organizational reliance on Django.
- A developer or OSPO champion needs to spend an annual OSS, security, developer-relations, recruiting, philanthropy, or community budget.
- A corporate membership or sponsorship comes up for renewal, changes tier, or loses its internal owner.
- New procurement, tax, sanctions, privacy, brand, or governance scrutiny requires the relationship to be revalidated.

**Recurring**

- Individuals reassess monthly or annual giving against income, competing causes, trust, and perceived connection.
- Companies inventory dependencies, review OSS strategy, allocate budgets, onboard or renew recipients, and report contribution or brand outcomes.
- Finance reconciles invoices/receipts and legal/compliance monitors terms, conflicts, and tax treatment.
- Funders review whether maintenance, security, community, and organizational capacity remain credible uses of support.

The triggers are **inferences; Medium confidence**. Platform mechanics and annual membership directly support recurring modes, but trigger prevalence among DSF donors is unknown.

## Desired outcomes

**Individual supporters** seek lower effort and uncertainty from intention to completed gift; confidence that the recipient is legitimate; a gift size and cadence sustainable within personal means; visible connection between collective funding and valued work; privacy and data handling consistent with preference; and a reason to continue without guilt or pressure.

**Corporate supporters/members** seek shorter internal approval and renewal time; explicit ownership of the relationship; a defensible link between the funding target and dependency, ecosystem, or brand objective; complete invoice, tax, vendor, legal, and benefit information; lower concentration and continuity risk in important maintenance; credible, non-exaggerated recognition; and evidence adequate for finance, leadership, employees, and community scrutiny.

**Both** seek more predictable resources for essential work, fewer unfunded maintenance bottlenecks, and accountability proportional to the gift. These are **directionally measurable inferences; Medium confidence**. A donation cannot by itself guarantee faster fixes, project longevity, security, support response, or influence; causal outcome targets require a defined funded program and baseline.

## Current behaviour or status quo

Individuals often encounter funding where development already happens. GitHub Sponsors connects funding to repositories and dependencies; a mixed-method study of 8,028 maintainers, 13,555 sponsors, and 22,515 sponsorships found sponsors mainly wanted to thank developers of software they used, but funding was long-tailed and generally insufficient for sustainable maintenance ([Zhang et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.13323), 26 November 2021). **Direct evidence; Medium confidence for GitHub, not all DSF giving.**

Companies more commonly contribute labor than cash. The 2024 OSS Funding Survey estimated 86% of responding organizations' contribution value was employee labor and 14% direct financial support; only 32% of respondents could state financial support by target, 22% the budget share, and 20% labor hours ([2024 Open Source Software Funding Report](https://opensourcefundingsurvey2024.com/), GitHub/Linux Foundation/Harvard collaborators, 19 November 2024). This shows measurement and ownership gaps, especially in decentralized organizations. **Direct survey evidence; Medium confidence** because extrapolation and respondent knowledge introduce uncertainty.

Corporate funding can be materially larger: GitHub reported that in 2022 organizations supplied nearly 40% of sponsorship funding and the average organization-funded sponsorship was nearly 15 times the average individual sponsorship ([“What's new with GitHub Sponsors,”](https://github.blog/open-source/maintainers/whats-new-with-github-sponsors/) 4 April 2023). GitHub added bulk sponsorship, invoicing, history, and budget dashboards, revealing the administrative jobs corporate funders need. **Direct platform data; Medium confidence; interested source.**

For the DSF, the 2024 impact report records $195,300 from corporate donors and $94,156 from individuals, with $229,893 of expenses on wages, $38,172 on grants, and $9,536 on professional fees/hosting. It links donations to two Fellows' maintenance/security/release work and to community grants ([DSF 2024 Annual Impact Report](https://www.djangoproject.com/foundation/reports/2024/), 30 June 2025). **Direct organizational reporting; High confidence for 2024 amounts, Medium for outcome attribution.**

## Pushes

- Essential maintenance is often unpaid or underpaid: 33% of 536 respondents to a Sovereign Tech Agency maintainer survey were unpaid but wanted payment, and about 30% were paid but not enough to live on ([survey findings](https://www.sovereign.tech/news/what-open-source-maintainers-shared-with-us), 9 October 2024). The sample was self-selected and should not be generalized to all maintainers.
- A company can incur private-fork, workaround, incident, delay, or replacement costs while the shared dependency remains under-resourced. Linux Foundation's 2026 ROI survey associates contribution with security, development, and talent benefits, but its modeled ROI should not be applied mechanically to a DSF membership ([ROI for Open Source Software Contribution](https://www.linuxfoundation.org/research/contribution-roi), February 2026).
- Individuals may recognize a mismatch between substantial personal benefit and zero payment, especially when they cannot contribute time.
- Security incidents, release pressure, maintainer turnover, and volunteer burnout make hidden maintenance visible.
- Corporate leaders increasingly ask OSPOs and engineering teams to make OSS use, risk, contribution, and reciprocity governable.
- Existing donors/members may see that lapsed funding would reduce capacity or force reprioritization.

## Pulls

- Gratitude and reciprocity tied to actual software use are directly evidenced in GitHub sponsorship behavior.
- A trusted nonprofit can pool small gifts into wages, grants, legal/administrative work, infrastructure, and community support that an individual cannot coordinate alone.
- Recurring support can better match recurring maintenance than episodic gratitude, although no reviewed evidence quantifies its effect for Django.
- DSF corporate membership offers a defined annual relationship, recognition benefits, and legal member rights; higher tiers currently add leadership access and visibility ([How Investing in Django Makes a Difference](https://www.djangoproject.com/sponsor/), updated June 2026).
- Direct sponsorship can target a maintainer or dependency; GitHub supports dependency discovery, bulk funding, invoices, and organizational dashboards.
- Corporate participation can serve business advantage, reputation, reciprocity, and founder/company values simultaneously; motivations need not be reduced to either altruism or ROI.
- Giving itself can create personal satisfaction. A statewide field experiment with 540,000 Alaskans found a donor-benefit (“warm glow”) appeal increased giving relative to control, while a recipient-benefit appeal did not increase average donation ([List et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-021-01095-8), *Nature Human Behaviour*, 12 April 2021). **Direct causal evidence for that campaign; Low-to-Medium transferability to technical communities.**

## Anxieties

**Individual donor:** whether a small gift matters; whether money reaches maintenance or community outcomes; whether “free” software really needs money; fraud or weak governance; unwanted publicity or solicitations; payment/privacy security; tax deductibility; and choosing between Django and other urgent causes.

**Technical/OSPO champion:** inability to prove organizational use or criticality; the proposal being dismissed as charity; choosing the wrong recipient layer; making claims about security or ROI that funding cannot guarantee; or losing credibility if the project later disappoints.

**Economic buyer/leadership:** unclear business relevance, precedent for funding every dependency, weak objectives, concentrated key-person risk, inability to compare options, or reputational exposure from the recipient or community.

**Finance/procurement:** no budget category, vendor onboarding effort, invoice currency and timing, tax status/deductibility, benefits that change accounting treatment, payment security, sanctions, refund/cancellation terms, and renewal ownership.

**Legal/compliance/brand:** membership rights and obligations, trademark/logo use, advertising claims, conflicts of interest, privacy, endorsement implications, undue influence, and whether a benefit compromises nonprofit or community independence.

**Community-facing corporate roles:** being perceived as buying influence, extracting marketing value, or claiming credit disproportionate to support.

General donor evidence makes these accountability concerns credible: Give.org's 2024 U.S. survey found respondents rated review of spending, appeal accuracy, donor-data protection, program/finance/governance information, achievements, and board oversight as important accountability elements ([“Public Priorities on Charity Accountability”](https://give.org/news/public-priorities-on-charity-accountability), 3 July 2024). **Direct general-donor evidence; Medium confidence for individuals, Low for corporate OSS buyers.**

## Habits and inertia

- Free access makes consumption easy and payment invisible; gratitude does not automatically become a recurring budget.
- Individuals default to one-time campaign gifts, familiar charities, or no action; low perceived efficacy and competing causes preserve inaction.
- Companies treat employee labor, bug reports, or upstream code as their contribution and may not create a cash-funding path.
- Decentralized dependency use obscures who owns the relationship; the funding survey directly documents weak knowledge of budgets and labor.
- Annual planning, procurement thresholds, vendor lists, cost centers, and renewal calendars favor existing recipients and disadvantage a new OSS payment.
- A champion's departure can orphan a membership even when technical dependence persists.
- Marketing, philanthropy, security, engineering, and OSPO budgets optimize for different outcomes, so funding stalls when the job is not classified.
- Public recognition tiers encourage continuity for some organizations but may make anonymous or purely stewardship-led funders see poor fit.

## Decision criteria

No evidence supports one universal ranking. Importance changes by supporter, motive, amount, relationship, and consequence.

| Criterion | Contextual importance | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Genuine connection to software used or mission valued | Strong gate for individuals and dependency-led companies | High |
| Recipient legitimacy, legal/tax status, payment security, privacy | Baseline for all; formal gate for corporate finance/legal | High |
| Clear funding target and additional-funds use | High when moving beyond symbolic gifts | High |
| Governance, board oversight, conflicts, and technical independence | High for trust and large/member relationships | High |
| Evidence of maintenance/community work and financial stewardship | High for renewal; metrics must match the funded job | High |
| Continuity, key-person, and concentration-risk plan | High for dependency stewardship | Medium-High |
| Relationship rights and obligations: gift, sponsor, member, event, in-kind | Gate because the labels are not interchangeable | High |
| Internal business relevance or mission alignment | High for corporate economic buyer | High |
| Amount, cadence, tier, fees, currency, invoice, cancellation, and accounting fit | Practical gate; importance rises with company size/spend | High |
| Proportionate recognition, audience relevance, and brand safety | High for brand/recruiting job; low for anonymous stewardship | Medium |
| Community autonomy and absence of pay-to-play technical privilege | Trust gate for donors, maintainers, and community | High |
| Comparable references or peer validation | Useful for corporate validation, not proof of outcome | Medium |

Give.org's impact research also cautions against a single definition or cross-charity score: respondents divided “impact” among outcomes, goals, counts, efficiency, and quality, and split 40%/37% on case-by-case versus comparative assessment ([2024 Donor Trust Special Report](https://give.org/docs/default-source/donor-trust-library/2024_summer_dtr_report_final.pdf), 2024). **Direct general-donor evidence; Medium confidence.**

## Main concerns

**Practical concerns:** identifying dependence and funding target; donation amount/cadence; eligibility; receipts/invoices; tax and accounting; workplace match; privacy; benefits; renewals; internal owner; usable reporting; and contact continuity.

**Legitimate limitations:** financial support cannot purchase a support SLA, guarantee project survival, dictate a roadmap, prevent every vulnerability, or establish causality between one gift and a release. DSF funding supports a portfolio of legal, financial, maintenance, governance, and community work. Direct maintainer funding may be more targeted but less institutionally diversified.

**Governance concerns:** donor/member influence, conflicts, board accountability, concentration of revenue, dependence on a few corporate funders, and preserving technical decision-making. Current Django governance states that the DSF handles financial/legal matters while the project and elected Steering Council manage technical development ([Django project organization](https://docs.djangoproject.com/en/6.0/internals/organization/), accessed 18 July 2026). **Direct policy; High confidence.**

**Organizational objections:** “we already contribute employee time”; “finance has no category”; “marketing cannot prove reach”; “security cannot treat a donation as a control”; “legal will not approve benefits or member rights”; “we cannot fund every transitive dependency”; or “leadership sees this as philanthropy outside strategy.”

**Misconceptions:** open source means costless maintenance; a donation buys prioritization; corporate membership is merely advertising; DSF individual membership can be purchased; sponsorship is always tax deductible; a logo proves responsible OSS practice; or cash is the only valuable form of contribution.

**Emotional resistance hypotheses:** guilt-based appeals can trigger avoidance; a developer may fear looking uncommercial internally; finance may distrust an unfamiliar recipient; and community members may resent corporate visibility. **Low confidence.**

## Objections and perceived risks

- **“Django is free, so volunteers have already chosen not to be paid.”** Contradicted by maintainer survey evidence and the paid Fellowship model. License freedom does not remove maintenance labor.
- **“Our company already pays vendors and engineers around Django.”** That may be substantial contribution, but it does not necessarily fund shared upstream maintenance or the DSF. The correct comparison requires mapping targets, not dismissing labor or cash.
- **“Funding the DSF guarantees faster security fixes or our feature.”** Unsupported. Funding adds capacity; technical governance remains separate and no donor-specific service promise follows.
- **“Corporate membership is only a sponsorship package.”** Incomplete. DSF pages attach recognition benefits, but current bylaws also define a renewable member relationship with voting rights. The practical scope of those rights needs clarification.
- **“Corporate membership gives our company technical influence.”** Contradicted by Django's separation of financial/legal and technical governance. Meetings or member voting are not roadmap control.
- **“Directly sponsoring a Fellow is the same as employing or directing them.”** Unsupported. A sponsored tier funds the role and offers recognition/meetings; employment, priorities, and technical authority remain governed by DSF/project arrangements.
- **“All sponsorships are charitable deductions.”** False as a general rule. GitHub says sponsorships are generally not deductible unless the recipient/status makes them so; finance/tax must assess the actual transaction.
- **“Visibility proves ROI.”** Unsupported. Reach, clicks, recruiting influence, avoided maintenance cost, and community trust are different outcomes with different evidence.
- **“A small individual gift cannot matter.”** Unsupported at the pooled-fund level, but no reviewed evidence quantifies a marginal DSF gift's effect. Avoid invented equivalences.
- **“A public sponsor list proves independence and accountability.”** Unsupported. It shows association; filings, governance, conflicts, spending, and results answer different questions.

## Information needed to make progress

**Individual donor:** the legal recipient; mission and funded work; one-time/recurring options; payment methods, fees, privacy and public-recognition choices; tax/receipt treatment by jurisdiction; how pooled funds are allocated; recent finances and results; cancellation; and employer-match eligibility.

**Corporate champion/evaluator:** which products and teams depend on Django; direct versus transitive criticality; what failure or under-maintenance would cost; current labor/cash/in-kind contribution; desired job (stewardship, reciprocity, brand, recruiting, community, or specific program); alternative recipients/instruments; requested amount/cadence; and evidence appropriate to that job.

**Economic buyer/approver:** strategic fit; budget owner and cost center; options and consequences of no funding; proportionality; renewal criteria; accountable internal owner; and whether rights/benefits create obligations.

**Finance/procurement/legal/brand:** legal entity and tax status; membership/sponsorship agreement; bylaws and voting rights; invoice/payment/currency; term, cancellation and renewal; benefit valuation; trademark and publicity permissions; data processing; conflicts/sanctions; financial statements/filings; and authorized signatories.

**Renewal review:** funds received and used; work/results against the stated purpose; governance or risk changes; relationship benefits actually used; current dependence; unresolved concerns; and next-period priorities. These are **information needs, not asset recommendations**.

## Trusted content formats

- **Primary legal and governance records**—bylaws, policies, agreements, board minutes, election rules, and technical-governance documents—establish rights, boundaries, and independence.
- **Public financial filings and reconciled annual financial/impact reporting** support spending and sustainability diligence. The DSF publishes an impact report and links its Form 990 filings.
- **Program-level work records** such as Fellow activity, releases, security process, grants, and funded-work reports connect money to activity without claiming individual-gift causality.
- **Dependency inventories and internal engineering evidence** make a corporate stewardship case more credible than popularity statistics.
- **A short internal proposal with relationship, target, amount, owner, risk, approval, and renewal test** is usable by corporate stakeholders; this is an inferred decision format, not a recommendation that Django produce it.
- **Invoices, receipts, tax statements, benefit schedules, and contact details** enable finance to complete the transaction.
- **Peer/reference conversations** with current members, OSPO leaders, engineers, and community representatives validate how the relationship behaves in practice.
- **Plain confirmation and periodic updates** can reinforce individual trust; long reports remain available for deeper diligence.

Format trust is **inferred; Medium confidence**, except donor demand for spending, governance, achievements, and accurate appeals, which Give.org supports directly.

## Discovery, evaluation, validation, and engagement channels

**Discovery:** individuals encounter funding through the DSF website, a campaign or annual report, GitHub sponsor surfaces, project/community discussion, conferences, newsletters, social posts, or workplace-giving portals. GitHub's repository/dependency integration is a direct discovery mechanism. Social and podcasts can create awareness, but no reviewed evidence shows that they drive DSF gifts. A familiar online giving platform can lend trust—Give.org reports 62% of platform users said presence on a well-known platform would increase trust—but this is general U.S. donor evidence ([Online Giving Platforms and Donor Expectations](https://give.org/donor-trust-report/2026-special-report-online-giving-platforms-donor-expectations), 16 June 2026).

Corporate discovery often starts with an engineer, dependency/security inventory, OSPO, peer company, executive value, developer-relations opportunity, conference relationship, or membership renewal. The Linux Foundation's OSPO guide shows programs can begin bottom-up from developers or top-down from management and commonly join engineering, legal, compliance, and executive review ([Creating an Open Source Program](https://www.linuxfoundation.org/resources/open-source-guides/creating-an-open-source-program), accessed 18 July 2026). **Direct practice guidance; Medium confidence.**

**Evaluation:** individuals use the funding explanation, annual impact/financial report, filings, recipient identity, and payment/privacy terms. Corporate roles add dependency data, bylaws, membership benefits, legal/tax terms, invoices, brand fit, budget evidence, and internal peer review. Docs and release notes evidence ongoing work but do not alone establish funding need or governance.

**Validation:** independent tax filings, board/technical governance records, public work history, current-member references, OSPO or engineering peer review, and reconciliation of claims across sources validate the recipient and relationship. GitHub/issues can validate activity; they cannot prove financial causality. Forums, Discord, mailing lists, conferences, and sprints can validate community reality through interaction, but no evidence ranks them as funder channels.

**Ongoing engagement:** annual reports, filings, board minutes, Fellow/work reports, release notes/advisories, newsletters, member meetings, community channels, conferences, and renewal conversations maintain trust and surface change. Short-form social is primarily awareness/recognition; professional peer networks help corporate champions validate; GitHub/dependency tooling helps corporate allocation and reporting. Claimed channel importance beyond these functions remains unmeasured.

## Decision-makers and influencers

**Individual donation:** the donor is initiator, evaluator, economic buyer, approver, payer, and consequence-bearer. A partner/household budget, tax adviser, employer-match platform, payment provider, or trusted peer may influence. No corporate funnel should be projected onto this decision.

**Corporate support/member decision:** roles vary and can collapse in a small company.

| Role | Likely occupant | Job and consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Initiator/champion | Django-using engineer, maintainer, engineering manager, OSPO, security leader, developer relations, founder | Makes dependence or opportunity visible; risks internal credibility and owns momentum |
| Researcher/evaluator | OSPO/program manager with engineering, security, community/DevRel | Verifies recipient, target, relationship, technical relevance, community fit, and options |
| Economic buyer/budget owner | CTO/VP Engineering, OSPO leader, CISO/security budget owner, marketing/DevRel/recruiting leader, founder; sometimes philanthropy/CSR | Decides whether the outcome justifies spend from their budget; bears opportunity cost |
| Finance/AP/procurement | Finance controller, accounts payable, procurement/vendor management | Onboards entity, validates invoice/payment/tax/accounting, executes and records spend |
| Legal/compliance/privacy/brand reviewer | Counsel, compliance, privacy, tax, communications/brand | Reviews member rights, terms, benefits, data, claims, trademark, conflicts, sanctions, and reputation |
| Final approver | Budget owner, executive, procurement authority, enterprise owner, board/committee depending threshold | Accepts organizational exposure and authorizes spend; GitHub enterprise owners can technically enable or block organization sponsorships |
| Relationship owner | OSPO, engineering program manager, DevRel/partnerships, or executive assistant/operations in a small firm | Uses benefits appropriately, receives updates, reports internally, and prepares renewal |
| Influencers/consequence-bearers | Product/application teams, security/SRE, maintainers, employees/candidates, customers, Django community | Experience dependency health, credibility, community impact, or perceived undue influence |

GitHub documentation directly proves enterprise-owner policy control, organizational invoicing, dashboards, and dependency-oriented sponsorship; the exact human approval map is a **researcher inference; Medium confidence**. Interviews are needed across small firms, enterprises with OSPOs, and non-technical corporate members.

## Appropriate next actions for Django to encourage

- **Complete a one-time or recurring individual donation** when the job is gratitude, belonging, or pooled continuity support.
- **Check and use employer matching/workplace giving** when the job is increasing personal support through an available company mechanism.
- **Identify where Django creates personal or organizational value before choosing a funding target** when the job is responsible allocation rather than generic generosity.
- **Open an internal stewardship discussion with the dependency owner, OSPO, engineering/security leadership, or budget owner** when the job is making reliance fundable.
- **Choose explicitly among an individual gift, DSF corporate membership, direct project/maintainer sponsorship, event sponsorship, staff time, or in-kind help** when the job is matching relationship and target to outcome.
- **Review legal rights, technical-governance boundaries, finances, and current work before a material commitment** when the job is accountability without control.
- **Name an internal relationship owner and renewal test** when the job is durable corporate support rather than a champion-dependent one-off.
- **Renew, adjust, or stop support based on current dependence, purpose, evidence, and means** when the job is ongoing stewardship.

These are audience progress actions, not campaign or asset recommendations.

## Overlaps with other audiences

- **Existing Django users and professional developers:** benefit and gratitude can trigger individual giving; their job as users remains distinct.
- **CTOs, engineering managers, architects, security and large-organization stakeholders:** they may champion, evaluate, buy, or approve dependency stewardship.
- **Startups and agencies/consultancies:** founders compress decision roles and may value both reliance and market reputation.
- **Ecosystem/core contributors and package maintainers:** they are potential recipients, evidence providers, and donors; funding power must not determine technical authority.
- **Educators and learners:** past benefit or access/community mission can motivate giving, but education remains the underlying job.
- **Event organizers and community leaders:** they may receive DSF grants and sell event sponsorship; their funding relationship is program-specific.
- **Corporate marketing, developer relations, recruiting, CSR, OSPO, finance, and legal:** each participates for a different internal job; “corporate decision-maker” hides this system.

## Possible segmentation problems

- **“Donor,” “sponsor,” and “member” are not synonyms.** DSF public copy currently mixes them; rights and accounting can differ materially.
- **“Individual member” is not an individual donor tier.** DSF membership recognizes contribution and carries voting rights.
- **Corporate motivation is multi-label.** Dependency resilience, reciprocity, security, brand, recruiting, founder values, and philanthropy can coexist; forcing one ROI story can misclassify the buyer.
- **Funding target matters more than company size alone.** DSF portfolio funding, a Fellow tier, a direct maintainer, an event, and in-kind infrastructure solve different jobs.
- **User is not automatically funder.** Heavy dependence can remain invisible; a sponsor can also support for mission or brand without using Django directly.
- **Small and large companies have structurally different funnels.** Role compression should not be mistaken for absence of finance/legal needs.
- **Visibility-seeking and anonymous supporters need different relationships.** Recognition is a pull for some and a deterrent for others.
- **A framework-standard decision is not a funding decision.** Currency, architecture, support, security, and hiring may justify reliance; funding adds stewardship, relationship, legal, and accountability criteria.
- **Geography changes tax, payment, privacy, sanctions, charitable-status, and giving norms.** Much donor evidence reviewed is U.S.-based.

## Unanswered research questions

1. What originally triggered first-time and recurring DSF gifts, and what causes lapse, upgrade, downgrade, or renewal?
2. How do individual donors divide among direct users, former learners, community/cause supporters, and workplace-match donors, across regions and gift sizes?
3. Which outcomes do DSF donors actually value—Fellowship maintenance/security/releases, grants/events, governance/legal work, community inclusion, or general continuity—and how do those priorities differ by segment?
4. How do current and prospective corporate members understand the difference among donation, sponsorship, and legal corporate membership, including voting rights?
5. Who has exercised DSF corporate-member voting rights, on what matters, and what safeguards or conflict rules govern corporate representatives?
6. What are the real approval paths, budget sources, cycle times, objections, and renewal tests in small firms, enterprises with OSPOs, and enterprises without them?
7. Which role is usually the effective champion—engineer, OSPO, security, DevRel/marketing, founder, or executive—and where do proposals stall?
8. What evidence makes dependency stewardship approvable without overstating ROI, security, or roadmap influence?
9. When do organizations prefer DSF portfolio funding, a Sponsored Fellow tier, direct maintainer sponsorship, event sponsorship, employee time, or in-kind support?
10. How concentrated is DSF revenue by funder, what renewal volatility exists, and how do supporters evaluate independence and concentration risk?
11. Which discovery and engagement channels actually precede donations or corporate inquiries, rather than merely receiving attention?
12. How do privacy, anonymity, tax treatment, payment methods, currency, and regional trust affect conversion and retention outside the United States?
13. How do maintainers and the wider Django community perceive corporate benefits, meetings, recognition, and member voting rights?
14. Which funded outcomes can be measured credibly at program level, and which should remain qualitative or contextual to avoid false attribution?
