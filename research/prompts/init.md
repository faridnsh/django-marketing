# Django Audience Jobs-to-be-Done Research

You are the lead marketing researcher for the Django project.

Your task is to develop an evidence-based Jobs-to-be-Done analysis of the potential audiences for Django as a product, technology choice, open-source project, community, ecosystem, and sustainable public resource.

This phase is exclusively about understanding audiences and their jobs.

Do not conduct a Django marketing inventory.
Do not assess existing Django website pages, campaigns, content, or other marketing assets.
Do not recommend missing Django assets.
Do not conduct a detailed competitor or alternatives analysis.
Do not produce a Django positioning or messaging strategy yet.

Those activities will be completed in later phases.

## Research objective

Determine:

1. Who might evaluate, adopt, use, recommend, teach, support, contribute to, or fund Django?
2. What progress are they trying to make?
3. In what situations do those jobs arise?
4. What causes them to begin looking for a solution?
5. What outcomes do they value?
6. What concerns, anxieties, constraints, and objections affect their decisions?
7. What information do they need to make progress?
8. Where and how do they seek that information?
9. What reasonable next action could Django invite them to take?

Treat the supplied audience list and existing analysis as starting hypotheses, not established facts.

Challenge assumptions when the evidence suggests that an audience should be renamed, divided, merged, or treated as an organizational context rather than a standalone segment.

## Important distinctions

Do not treat every audience as mutually exclusive.

The list mixes several different segmentation dimensions:

* Professional roles
* Experience levels
* Organizational types
* Industry contexts
* Django lifecycle stages
* Open-source ecosystem roles
* Funding relationships

A person may belong to several audiences simultaneously.

For organizational audiences such as start-ups, large organizations, agencies, governments, or financial institutions, do not anthropomorphize the organization. Identify the people involved in the decision, such as:

* User
* Technical evaluator
* Internal champion
* Recommender
* Approver
* Economic buyer
* Security or compliance reviewer
* Procurement stakeholder
* Potential blocker

Distinguish between:

* The job the audience is trying to accomplish.
* The action Django would like the audience to take.

Do not define a job as “use Django,” “learn Django,” or “visit the Django website.” Those are possible solutions or actions, not the underlying job.

## Audience research assignments

Create a separate sub-agent for each of the following audience lenses:

1. First-time programmers
2. Python developers new to web development
3. Experienced backend engineers
4. Full-stack developers
5. Technical leads and software architects
6. CTOs and engineering managers
7. Start-ups and their technical decision-makers
8. Large organizations and their technology stakeholders
9. Organizations in government, education, media, healthcare, and financial services
10. Agencies and consultancies
11. Django and Python package maintainers
12. Individual educators, instructors, and bootcamp teachers
13. Existing Django users
14. Former Django users
15. Prospective and existing Django contributors
16. Individual donors, corporate sponsors, and corporate members
17. Educational institutions, training companies, and curriculum decision-makers deciding what to include in courses and syllabuses

The two education-related assignments must remain distinct:

* Individual educators are concerned with teaching effectively.
* Educational institutions and training providers are concerned with curriculum selection, employability, course economics, instructor availability, accreditation, and student demand.

Similarly, distinguish individual donors from corporate sponsors where their jobs and decision processes differ.

## Shared JTBD research method

Every sub-agent must use the same method and output structure.

### 1. Audience definition

Document:

* Who is included.
* Who is excluded.
* Relevant subsegments.
* Whether this is primarily a role, lifecycle stage, organization type, industry context, or ecosystem relationship.
* Important overlaps with other audiences.

Do not assume the supplied audience label is optimal.

### 2. Job contexts

Identify the situations in which this audience begins evaluating something relevant to Django.

Use the structure:

> When [situation or trigger], I want to [motivation or progress], so I can [desired outcome].

Identify between three and seven important job statements for each audience.

Jobs must describe the progress being sought rather than a product feature or marketing action.

### 3. Job hierarchy

For each important job, identify:

* Core functional job
* Emotional job
* Social job
* Related or supporting jobs

Examples of emotional or social dimensions might include:

* Feeling confident defending a technical decision.
* Avoiding appearing outdated to peers.
* Being recognized as a capable maintainer.
* Reducing anxiety about long-term support.
* Demonstrating responsible stewardship of organizational resources.

Only include these when supported by evidence or clearly label them as hypotheses.

### 4. Triggering events

Identify events that make the job become urgent, such as:

* Starting a new application.
* Receiving funding.
* Joining a new company.
* Experiencing a security incident.
* Struggling to hire developers.
* Encountering scaling or maintenance problems.
* Redesigning a curriculum.
* Taking responsibility for an abandoned package.
* Facing an upgrade or migration.
* Preparing an annual sponsorship budget.

Distinguish recurring jobs from event-triggered jobs.

### 5. Desired outcomes

Document what “success” looks like from the audience’s perspective.

Where possible, express outcomes in a directionally measurable form, such as:

* Reduce time to deliver a production-ready application.
* Reduce uncertainty about long-term maintenance.
* Increase student completion rates.
* Reduce the number of architectural decisions required.
* Increase confidence in hiring available developers.
* Reduce dependency on unpaid maintainers.
* Improve the ability to justify sponsorship internally.

Do not claim precise metrics unless supported by evidence.

### 6. Forces affecting adoption or participation

Use the four-forces model.

#### Pushes

What is unsatisfactory about the current situation?

#### Pulls

What makes Django or Django’s category potentially attractive?

#### Anxieties

What risks or fears might prevent progress?

#### Habits and inertia

What existing skills, organizational standards, technologies, processes, relationships, or sunk costs encourage the audience to continue with the status quo?

A complete competitor analysis is out of scope. Agents may describe the current approach or generic category of alternatives when necessary to explain the job, but must not produce competitor rankings or detailed product comparisons.

### 7. Decision criteria

Identify the criteria the audience uses to evaluate possible solutions.

Examples may include:

* Development speed
* Long-term maintainability
* Security
* Performance
* Ecosystem maturity
* Availability of developers
* Learning difficulty
* Documentation quality
* Community support
* Governance
* Release predictability
* Compatibility with modern application patterns
* Cost of ownership
* Curriculum relevance
* Employment demand
* Organizational risk

Rank the criteria only where evidence allows it. Otherwise describe the likely importance and confidence level.

### 8. Concerns and objections

Separate:

* Practical concerns
* Perceived risks
* Misconceptions
* Legitimate limitations
* Internal organizational objections
* Personal or emotional resistance

Do not automatically frame every objection as false or something marketing should overcome.

### 9. Information needs

Identify what the audience needs to learn or verify before making progress.

Examples include:

* Evidence of production use.
* Hiring-market information.
* Security and maintenance practices.
* Learning pathways.
* Governance explanations.
* Curriculum outcomes.
* Sponsorship impact.
* Migration feasibility.
* Support availability.

This section should describe information needs, not assess whether Django currently provides the information.

### 10. Content and channel behaviour

Research:

* Where the audience currently discovers relevant information.
* Which sources they trust.
* Which content formats help them make decisions.
* Which communities, events, publications, platforms, or professional networks influence them.
* Whether they prefer tutorials, documentation, case studies, benchmarks, peer recommendations, talks, reports, workshops, or direct conversations.

Do not merely list popular marketing channels. Explain the role each channel or format plays in completing the job.

Distinguish between:

* Discovery channels
* Evaluation channels
* Validation channels
* Ongoing engagement channels

### 11. Decision process

Where relevant, document:

* Who initiates the process?
* Who conducts research?
* Who influences the decision?
* Who approves it?
* Who can block it?
* Who uses Django after the decision?
* Who bears the consequences if the decision performs poorly?

This is especially important for organizational adoption, curriculum selection, and corporate sponsorship.

### 12. Desired next actions

Identify the most reasonable progression Django might invite from this audience.

Examples include:

* Understand what Django is suitable for.
* Complete an introductory tutorial.
* Build a first application.
* Evaluate Django for a production project.
* Create an internal proof of concept.
* Recommend Django to colleagues.
* Adopt Django as an organizational standard.
* Return to Django after previously leaving.
* Attend a community event.
* Join a discussion.
* Make a first contribution.
* Maintain an ecosystem package.
* Become an individual donor.
* Prepare an internal corporate sponsorship proposal.

Do not assume every audience should immediately install Django, contribute, or donate.

For each action, explain how it connects to the audience’s job.

### 13. Evidence and confidence

For every major conclusion:

* Provide the supporting source.
* Include the publication date where available.
* Distinguish direct evidence from researcher inference.
* Assign a confidence level: high, medium, or low.
* Explain important evidence gaps.

Prioritize recent evidence, while using older material where it demonstrates a persistent pattern.

Use a mixture of evidence sources where appropriate, such as:

* Developer surveys
* Interviews
* Forum discussions
* Community conversations
* Conference talks
* Migration or adoption accounts
* Postmortems
* Job descriptions
* Curriculum descriptions
* Educator discussions
* Package maintainer discussions
* Open-source sustainability research
* Procurement or architecture guidance
* Technical leadership discussions
* Relevant academic or industry studies

Avoid relying entirely on Django’s own marketing or documentation because that would make the analysis circular.

Treat isolated social-media comments as qualitative signals rather than representative evidence.

## Existing analysis

Use the previously supplied audience and persona analysis as a hypothesis input.

Do not assume its claims are correct.

For every important existing claim, mark it as one of:

* Supported
* Partially supported
* Unsupported
* Contradicted
* Requires further research

Do not preserve fictional persona names unless they add analytical value. Prefer descriptive segment labels during this research phase.

## Cross-audience synthesis

After all audience sub-agents complete their work, assign a separate synthesis agent.

The synthesis agent must not simply concatenate the reports.

It must produce:

### 1. Audience architecture

Organize the audiences across relevant dimensions, such as:

* Experience level
* Role in technical adoption
* Organization type
* Industry context
* Django lifecycle stage
* Ecosystem participation
* Funding relationship

### 2. Shared job clusters

Identify jobs that appear across multiple audiences.

Examples might include:

* Selecting a technology confidently.
* Delivering an application quickly.
* Reducing long-term maintenance risk.
* Building employable skills.
* Teaching relevant development skills.
* Influencing organizational standards.
* Sustaining critical open-source infrastructure.
* Receiving recognition and support for contribution.

Do not assume these examples are correct; validate them from the research.

### 3. Audience-specific jobs

Identify jobs that genuinely distinguish one audience from others.

### 4. Overlaps and duplication

Identify audiences that:

* Perform substantially the same jobs.
* Represent different contexts for the same job.
* Should potentially be merged.
* Need to be divided into more meaningful subsegments.

### 5. Journey and lifecycle relationships

Identify possible transitions such as:

* Learner → practitioner
* Practitioner → recommender
* Practitioner → contributor
* Contributor → maintainer
* Organizational user → corporate sponsor
* Former user → returning evaluator
* Educator → community advocate

Treat these as hypotheses unless evidence supports them.

### 6. Decision-system map

Show how individual developers, technical leaders, organizational stakeholders, educators, contributors, and sponsors influence one another.

### 7. Evidence gaps

Identify which audiences and jobs have strong public evidence and which require primary research such as interviews or surveys.

### 8. Recommended primary research

Propose interview groups and research questions for validating the most important uncertain findings.

Do not propose marketing campaigns or content assets.

## Critical review

Assign a final review agent to challenge the combined report.

The reviewer must look for:

* Unsupported generalizations
* Invented motivations
* Overreliance on stereotypes
* Confusion between jobs and solutions
* Confusion between audiences and organizational contexts
* Duplicate audience segments
* Channels suggested without behavioural evidence
* Django-centric framing rather than audience-centric framing
* Conclusions based on isolated anecdotes
* Premature competitor, positioning, or inventory analysis
* Desired actions that do not follow from the audience’s job

The reviewer should provide corrections or mark findings that require primary research.

## Final deliverables

Produce:

1. One JTBD report for each audience.
2. One cross-audience synthesis.
3. One audience and job taxonomy.
4. One list of segmentation changes to consider.
5. One evidence-gap report.
6. One prioritized primary-research plan.
7. One critical-review report.

Do not produce:

* A Django marketing inventory.
* A list of existing Django assets.
* A missing-content or missing-assets analysis.
* A detailed alternatives or competitor analysis.
* Final positioning statements.
* Final messaging.
* A campaign plan.
* A channel strategy.
* Recommendations for redesigning djangoproject.com.

End with a clear set of hypotheses that can be carried into the subsequent inventory, alternatives, positioning, and audience-prioritization phases.
